# 📊 Hotel Booking Data Analysis 



## 1. Introduction
The hospitality industry relies heavily on data-driven insights to improve customer satisfaction, optimize operations, and maximize revenue.  

This project analyzes hotel booking data to understand customer behavior, booking trends, cancellation patterns, and revenue performance.

The goal is to identify key patterns that can support strategic decision-making and improve overall business performance in hotels.

---

## 2. Data Source and Tools Used

### 📂 Data Source
- Dataset obtained from Kaggle (Hotel Booking Demand Dataset)

### 🛠 Tools Used
- Microsoft Excel  
- Pivot Tables  
- Charts (Column, Bar, Pie, Line, Doughnut)  
- Slicers for interactive filtering  

### 🧮 Calculated Columns
- **Estimated Revenue** = Average Daily Rate (ADR) × Total Nights  
- **Stay Duration** = Weekend Nights + Weekday Nights  
- **Traveler Type** = Classified as *Solo* or *Group* using an IF function  

---

## 3. ❓ Research Questions
The analysis is guided by the following key questions:

- Which hotel type records the highest number of bookings?  
- Which months experience the highest booking demand?  
- What proportion of total bookings are canceled?  
- How does lead time influence booking cancellation rates?  
- Which customer type contributes the highest estimated revenue?  
- How does revenue vary across different months?  
- How does stay duration vary across hotel types and seasons?  
- What is the distribution of travelers (solo vs group bookings)?  

---

## 4. 📊 Data Analysis and Findings

### 4.1 Hotel Booking Distribution
City Hotels record a higher number of bookings compared to Resort Hotels.  
This suggests stronger customer preference, possibly due to accessibility, pricing, and suitability for short stays.

---

### 4.2 Booking Demand by Month
Booking activity varies across the year, with **August** recording the highest number of bookings.  
This reflects peak seasonal demand influenced by holidays and travel trends.

---

### 4.3 Cancellation Analysis
Approximately **37.02%** of bookings are canceled.  
This indicates that over one-third of reservations do not result in actual stays, affecting occupancy and revenue planning.

---

### 4.4 Lead Time and Cancellation Relationship
Longer lead times are associated with higher cancellation rates.  
Customers who book far in advance are more likely to cancel than those booking closer to their stay date.

---

### 4.5 Revenue by Customer Type
**Transient customers** generate the highest estimated revenue.  
This highlights the importance of individual and short-term travelers to hotel profitability.

---

### 4.6 Revenue Trends by Month
Revenue fluctuates across months, with **July and August** generating the highest revenue.  
This aligns with peak booking periods and strong seasonal demand.

---

### 4.7 Stay Duration Patterns
- Resort Hotels → Longer stays (vacation-oriented)  
- City Hotels → Shorter stays (business/short visits)  

Seasonality also plays a role in influencing stay duration.

---

### 4.8 Traveler Type Distribution
Group travelers make up the majority of bookings, while solo travelers account for a smaller proportion.  
This suggests hotels primarily cater to families and group-based customers.

---

## 5. 🚀 Conclusion and Recommendations

The findings highlight key patterns in customer behavior, seasonal demand, and revenue generation.

### 💡 Recommendations
- Focus on high-demand segments such as transient and group travelers  
- Implement strategies to reduce cancellations (e.g., deposits, reminders)  
- Apply dynamic pricing during peak months  
- Develop packages to encourage longer stays  
- Optimize staffing and resources during high-demand periods  

---

## 📌 Summary
By leveraging these insights, hotels can:
- Improve operational efficiency  
- Enhance customer experience  
- Increase overall profitability
